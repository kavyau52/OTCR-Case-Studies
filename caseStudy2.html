01  /  Context

The client needed a rigorous baseline before any improvement roadmap could be built

Professional sports venues have become an increasingly visible front in corporate sustainability. Sponsors, certification bodies, and fans are applying pressure, and the gap between stated commitments and measurable performance carries real reputational and financial consequences for a franchise.

The client, a Major League Baseball team operating one of the country's most historic stadiums, had sustainability initiatives in place but lacked a structured view of how those initiatives measured up against peer organizations. Data was siloed across multiple platforms. Reporting units were inconsistent across categories. Several key domains had no baseline data at all. The engagement mandate was clear: conduct a rigorous benchmarking analysis, surface material gaps, and deliver an actionable framework for ongoing performance management.

"The client came to us to benchmark their metrics utilized to identify the impact of their sustainability initiatives and provide recommendations."

Scope of engagement
  • Energy consumption and GHG emissions
  • Water conservation across fixture, irrigation, and cooling tower
  • Waste diversion and landfill management
  • Identification of certification pathways (LEED, TRUE, ISO 20121)
OTCR methodology
  1. Benchmarked sustainability practices across sports and related industries
  2. Conducted data gap analysis across all four initiative areas
  3. Collected and standardized existing metrics from Dynegy and GOAL platforms
  4. Evaluated feasibility and impact of each recommendation
  5. Built implementation roadmaps with short and long-term timelines
  6. Developed guides for ongoing monitoring and reporting
02  /  Scorecard

Two domains above industry standard, two with material performance gaps

OTCR assessed the client's performance across four sustainability domains using a standardized scoring framework. Energy and carbon tracking demonstrated institutional discipline and above-average data maturity. Water and waste management revealed a different picture: significant data collection gaps, non-standard reporting units, and performance metrics well below the thresholds required for leading certification bodies.

03  /  Energy & Carbon

Data infrastructure exists, but granularity is insufficient to drive decisions

The client scores above industry standards on historical data availability and unit consistency. However, the data is too aggregated to support targeted intervention. Energy meters on Dynegy carry numerical identifiers that have not been mapped to physical locations or system types. Carbon emissions are reported as totals without source-level breakdowns. The two platforms in use, Dynegy and GOAL, produce conflicting figures for the same reporting periods.

On a performance basis, the gap is significant. At 23.7 kWh per square foot, the client is consuming roughly 48% more electricity than the related industry average of 16 kWh. Carbon emissions per square foot are 4.3 kg above peer benchmarks. These are not marginal variances.

04  /  Water

A reported 13% reduction, but insufficient data fidelity to validate the result

Water management is where data collection deficiencies are most acute. The client reported a 13% reduction in total water usage between 2021 and 2022. Under normal circumstances that would represent a meaningful efficiency gain. The issue is that the underlying data lacks the categorization and coverage to distinguish genuine conservation from incomplete capture.

Industry best practice requires tracking plumbing fixture, irrigation, and cooling tower as separate line items. The client currently aggregates all water consumption without source labeling. Cooling tower data, a required input for most major certification frameworks, does not exist. As OTCR noted in the analysis: "it's difficult to conclude their current performance on water consumption with limited data."

Plumbing & fixture
Estimated 13.5% reduction, 2021-2022

Industry standard tracks fixture water as a discrete category, targeting 30% reduction from the established baseline. The client's reported figure is an estimate and may not be representative of actual consumption. Submeter installation would convert this into a defensible, auditable data point.

Near-term path: float method to gather preliminary flow data, followed by dedicated submeter installation within a 1-2 year horizon
Irrigation
No data currently available

Irrigation typically represents the largest single source of water consumption at a venue. Industry standard tracks it as a discrete category, targeting 50-70% reduction from regional baseline. The client has no data on record for this category.

Target: halve landscaping and irrigation consumption; deploy flow meters and moisture sensors within a 2-3 month implementation window
Cooling tower
No data. No viable certification path without it.

Cooling tower management is a mandatory reporting input for most major sustainability certification frameworks. Bleed-off rate is tracked in gallons per minute per BTU. Integrating cooling tower management into the sustainability program's scope is the foundational step before any certification pursuit.

Industry protocol: monthly reporting on consumption and bleed-off rate; annual water quality report at minimum
05  /  Waste

93% of waste goes to landfill. Closing that gap is the central value creation opportunity.

Waste management represents the most significant performance gap in the engagement. The client currently diverts 7% of total waste from landfills. LEED Platinum and TRUE certification both require 90%. Every direct and indirect benchmark organization OTCR analyzed was performing at or above that standard.

Current waste diversion rate
7%
In 2022, the client recycled 132.35 tons from a total of 2,040.06 tons generated. The Philadelphia Eagles divert 99% of waste from landfill. The Atlanta Falcons and SF Giants both exceed 90%. Hilton, Coca-Cola, and AT&T all operate above the 90% threshold as well.
Compounding the gap: the client tracks only annual totals, lacks monthly data points, and reports in short tons rather than the metric tons required for most certification submissions.
Industry standard for LEED certification
90%
TRUE and LEED certification bodies both define 90% as the minimum diversion threshold for recognition. Seven other MLB franchises currently hold stadium-level LEED certification. The gap between 7% and 90% is material but addressable through targeted capital deployment.
The Eagles' onsite biodigester was cited as the primary driver of their LEED Gold Certification, diverting approximately 4,000 tons of waste from landfills annually.

06  /  Prioritization

OTCR evaluated each initiative against impact potential and implementation feasibility

Each recommendation was scored across two dimensions: magnitude of impact on sustainability performance outcomes, and implementation feasibility given capital requirements, timeline, and organizational bandwidth. Four initiatives landed in the high-impact, high-feasibility quadrant and represent the strongest near-term ROI on program investment.

07  /  Recommendations

Three strategic priorities to drive near-term performance gains

Based on the gap analysis, peer benchmarking, and feasibility matrix, OTCR distilled the engagement into three prioritized recommendations. Each is grounded in specific data from the analysis, has a defined outcome, and can be initiated without large upfront capital commitments.

Recommendation 01
Establish data integrity as the foundation for all performance improvement

The client cannot credibly measure sustainability performance, let alone improve it, until the underlying data infrastructure is standardized. Reconcile the platform discrepancy between Dynegy and GOAL, map every energy meter to a physical location and system type, and convert waste reporting units from short tons to metric tons. These are low-cost, high-leverage interventions that unlock every downstream analysis and certification submission.

Initiate with energy meters: transfer numerical identifiers to Excel, assign descriptive labels, and categorize by system pattern. Target completion within 1-2 months with no capital outlay.
Recommendation 02
Deploy waste diversion infrastructure to close the gap to certification thresholds

Moving from 7% to 90% diversion requires operational change at the concessions level. The Philadelphia Eagles' experience provides a direct proof of concept: aerobic digestion diverted approximately 4,000 tons of waste per year and was the primary driver of their LEED Gold Certification. Installing a food waste digester for pre- and post-game periods, combined with compostable packaging sourced from a supplier like NatureWorks, represents the highest-ROI path to closing the performance gap.

Issue an RFP to Power Knot for an LFC-2000 quote. In parallel, pilot compostable serviceware in one concessions section to generate contamination and fan compliance data before full rollout.
Recommendation 03
Align public commitments with peer benchmarks to drive accountability and stakeholder credibility

Hilton has publicly committed to a 50% landfill waste reduction by 2030. The Atlanta Falcons and SF Giants both publish annual diversion rates. The client has not disclosed sustainability targets publicly. Establishing attainable, time-bound waste diversion milestones creates internal accountability and gives sponsors, certification bodies, and fans a concrete performance record to evaluate. The data infrastructure built through Recommendations 01 and 02 makes those commitments credible and auditable.

Commit to a 2025 target of 40% waste diversion, a 2027 target of 70%, and a 2030 target of 90%. Publish progress annually through a formal sustainability report, with GOAL as the system of record.
