01  /  Context

The client needed a structured view of a fast-moving space

Augmented and virtual reality were, for a long time, treated as peripheral curiosities in healthcare. That changed. By 2023, the FDA had cleared dozens of AR/VR products for clinical use, ranging from surgical planning software to neurology assessment tools to physical rehabilitation platforms. The pace of approvals was picking up, and the client, a global firm advising life sciences companies on emerging technology, needed to know exactly where the market stood.

Specifically, they needed a clear picture of which products had cleared regulatory hurdles, where the concentration of activity was, and where gaps remained. They also needed that picture to stay current, not go stale in a drawer somewhere.

"Lots of surgical planning tools, but also cool phobia treatments." The range of cleared applications was wider than most people assumed going in.

What the client needed
  • A complete inventory of FDA-cleared AR/VR products in life sciences
  • Use-case distribution across medical specialties
  • Identification of companies worth watching for partnership or M&A
  • A monitoring framework maintainable by an internal team
How OTCR approached it
  1. Reviewed client AR/VR advisory offerings to establish a baseline
  2. Researched FDA approval pathways for software-based LS devices
  3. Compiled adjacent, non-cleared applications used in LS contexts
  4. Designed and built a dynamic Excel tracker with standardized fields
  5. Populated the tracker with the full research corpus
  6. Automated analysis outputs using Excel macros
02  /  Deliverable

A living product tracker with 40+ FDA-cleared entries

The core deliverable was a macro-enabled Excel tracker cataloguing every FDA-cleared AR/VR product relevant to life sciences, capturing ten standardized dimensions per entry. The design prioritized longevity: new rows append without disrupting the macro logic, and an embedded legend reduces onboarding time for anyone picking it up later.

The tracker is organized by approval date and covers product area, care setting, end-user type, and a functional capability classification that distinguishes between standalone tools and those designed to complement existing clinical workflows. That last column turned out to be one of the more revealing fields in the dataset.

03  /  Market Analysis

What the data actually showed

The visualization work surfaced a few patterns worth paying attention to, some expected and some not. The most striking was the end-user split: the overwhelming majority of cleared products were built for clinicians, not patients. That skew has real implications for where the next wave of investment is likely to come from.

04  /  Adjacent Landscape

Beyond FDA clearance: three use cases already gaining traction

The tracker focused on cleared products, but a parallel research stream looked at life sciences applications where AR/VR is being used without FDA clearance. The picture here is different: less concentrated around imaging and surgical planning, and more oriented toward accessibility and patient experience. Although VR and AR are primarily used to help medical professionals diagnose patients, they can also be used to make healthcare more accessible and efficient post-diagnosis.

Use Case 01
Telemedicine

Virtual environments allow patients to participate in game-like diagnostic simulations equivalent to in-person assessments, with feedback delivered across multiple sensory modes. This is particularly relevant for patients who cannot travel to specialist facilities.

The model works on any online platform and does not require dedicated hardware on the patient side.

Live example: XRHealth, with three FDA-registered applications in addition to broader telehealth offerings
Use Case 02
Immersive Patient Education

Rather than static pamphlets or two-dimensional diagrams, interactive 3D simulations let patients see the effects of their condition and understand the proposed treatment in spatial terms. Clinicians use the same environment to demonstrate procedures.

Early implementations found this approach correlated with improved patient confidence and treatment adherence.

Live example: UbiSim
Use Case 03
Mindfulness and Stress Reduction

Personalized VR environments incorporating professional guidance, music, and visual feedback are being used to address individual anxiety patterns and phobias, including treatment-related anxiety in oncology settings.

Real-time metrics allow clinicians to adjust interventions and track whether they are having the intended effect.

Accessible via wellness and clinic platforms without a cleared device requirement

06  /  Recommendations

Three things the client should do with this

The tracker and analysis were designed to support decision-making, not just describe the landscape. Based on the patterns in the data, the team came away with three concrete recommendations for the client's advisory practice.

Recommendation 01
Target the patient-facing gap before someone else does

Roughly 83% of cleared products serve clinicians, not patients. That skew reflects the history of the market, not its future direction. Patient-facing and standalone products are underrepresented, and the adjacent use case research shows real clinical demand. The window for first-mover positioning in patient-direct AR/VR is open, but it will not stay open indefinitely as the market matures.

Prioritize partnerships with home-use innovators, particularly in ophthalmic and physical medicine, before the segment consolidates.
Recommendation 02
Stay focused on complement software as the core investment thesis

Nearly 79% of cleared products are complement technology, not standalone systems. That is not a coincidence. Software that layers on existing clinical infrastructure faces lower regulatory friction, faster development cycles, and stronger early returns than hardware-dependent standalone solutions. The data suggest this is where capital has been going, and where the track record of FDA approval is most consistent.

Weight the advisory portfolio toward software-first approaches, particularly in surgical planning and imaging where the cleared product concentration is highest.
Recommendation 03
Use the tracker to hold ground in Radiology and Orthopedics while watching the edges

Radiology and Orthopedics together represent roughly 69% of all cleared products. These are the established core of medtech AR/VR and will drive the bulk of near-term deal flow. The tracker is set up to monitor new approvals in these verticals automatically. At the same time, the adjacency research points to neurology and behavioral health as likely next-wave growth areas, which currently show up as small slices in the distribution chart.

Maintain depth in core verticals via the live tracker; assign a secondary workstream to neurology and behavioral health for the next engagement cycle.
